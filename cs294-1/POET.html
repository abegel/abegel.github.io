Andrew Begel

CS294-1 Reading Reviews

May 6, 2002

 

The Psychology of Everyday Things – Don Norman

 

This book is quite famous, and has made the rounds of many group meetings and classes that I’ve been in. The major point is that all items have identifying marks that indicate how they’re to be used. These marks are called affordances. An example is a door with a flat panel on it, which indicates that you should push it there to open the door. In addition, for devices whose purpose is not entirely transparent, and whose function does not afford direct manipulation, there exists a mapping between form and function. In some cases, this mapping can be obvious (e.g. the Mercedes seat adjuster), but in most, it’s not. Don Norman gives many clever examples of affordances and devices that fail to meet a user’s expectations, leading to much confusion. In that, he’s written a very well-supported article that is easy to understand. However, he seems unfairly biased against the kinds of mappings found in computer interfaces, or pretty much in any technology, where constraints on the physical interface trump all other constraints. Yes, the big idea in a computer is that one little square of silicon can do anything at all – the physical container does not expose the possibilities or afford any ideas on how to use it. Perhaps in later chapters he expounds on software user interfaces, but in this one he merely seems to dismiss non-physically-based interfaces as belonging to the class of hard-to-use items.

 

Contextual Design – Beer and Holtzblatt

 

This paper describes Contextual Design, a design methodology for user interface designers that embeds them in the life of their users in the hopes that they begin to understand the user’s world, before allowing them to begin designing. I think James Landay uses this design methodology in CS160. It appears that ethnographic studies of this sort are currently in vogue in HCI, however unlike XP in the software engineering world, these ideas actually seem to be grounded in reality. Observing your users, modeling their work flow and consolidating the ideas (into an abstraction) borrows extensively from anthropology; work redesign with a team is heavily adapted from management principles and rhetoric and public policy, finally, user interface design and paper mockups (lo-fi prototypes) have always been a standby of traditional HCI. I agree completely with this paper, however, there are problems with the length of time needed to perform the technique properly. An ethnographic study, while revealing to the utmost degree, is also time-intensive; if this is your team’s only full-time job, then that’s fine. If not, you need shortcuts to get good data. Luckily, the research community has explored this idea (I’ve read one paper in Jen Mankoff’s Assistive Technology class called Rapid Ethnography: Time Deepening Strategies for HCI Field Research (Daniel Millen, CUU 2000)). Otherwise the field of HCI might become all about anthropology and no one would ever get to the good stuff on the computer.
