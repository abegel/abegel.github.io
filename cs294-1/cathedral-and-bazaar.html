Andrew Begel

CS294-1 Reading Review

April 24, 2002

 

The Cathedral and the Bazaar and the Future of Cygnus Solutions: An Entrepreneur’s Account (Open Sources: Voices from the Open Source Revolution).

 

I suppose that the really interesting things that first started to dawn on me when Kirk McKusik came to speak and has continued with the two essays above, is that open source is an invented concept (not a found one) and is perceived by those involved in it to be a revolution in software development. From my original perspective as an academic who was continually frustrated with publicly available tools (read GNU) and admired vendor-provided tools as much higher quality but much higher cost, to say that I’m experiencing a complete revision of historical perspective is saying it gently. All the development processes I had taken for granted: open source code, open CVS repositories, an amorphous set of developers, an amorphous set of kvetchers, a set of mailing lists to keep track of development, bugs and use of the software, code that is not tightly held by a small group of designers – seem to be invented notions proffered by Stallman, McKusik, Raymond as solutions to their perception of software development at the time: non-accessible source code, tightly held design, hidden design criteria and test suites, poor quality software, a fixed set of paid developers loyal to the company, bug mailing lists that might as well be /dev/null, and unresponsive company management. Wow!

If we accept the open-source ideals as gospel, what implications ought it have for software development? First, they don’t claim this will work for all software. Specifically, Raymond suggests there has to be a kernel of code that already works and people are interested in that was developed in a small team privately and then released to the world. Once that code builds up a critical mass of users (and detractors), open-sourcing it just taps into the growing demand for changes that are beyond the means of the original developers. Second, if a project is open-source, wouldn’t that mean that no one could make any money from it? Well, here’s the neat thing. According to Tiemann, of Cygnus Solutions, that’s not true. Since most clients of open-source code would have been clients of the equivalent vendor-provided binaries, these same clients are completely willing to pay money for their software, because they perceive the software to provide them some benefit without having to waste their own engineers on it. This means that if there’s a company out there that is claiming credit for large portions of the open source code base for a particular project that some other company wants to use (and most likely customize for their usage), that company is likely to be found and paid by potential clients. The only catch here, which is what makes this subversive, is that all code developed by Cygnus (a prototypical example of an open-source company) is donated, free, back to the open source for all to use. So while the work by Cygnus is paid for by clients who want more functionality, the functionality itself is free to spread. That’s just amazing. Tiemann quotes Jefferson, “Information, no matter how expensive to create, can be replicated and shared at little or no cost.”

I now understand where the open-source movement is coming from, and how it can fit into the capitalist society that we’ve got, but where does it need to go? It relies heavily on volunteerism, people who have free time and feel like contributing. In this sense, it’s no different from a temple activity group, or the Lions club, only it’s for geeks. But is it reasonable to expect that there will always be a volunteer base, or will most development have to be taken over by companies like Cygnus (now owned by Redhat)?

There are other deficiencies in the open source model as well. UI design is said to not be found at all in open source software. Linux is ugly. Gnome and KDE are poor facsimiles of Windows, which is already considered to be a UI nightmare. Why doesn’t UI design work in an open source world. It’s because UI design isn’t about coding; it’s not about the man-machine interface – it’s about the human-human interface. Humans need to talk to other humans and watch other humans when they use the computer in order to gain any insight into how to improve an interface. Coders are far more comfortable talking to computers in abstractions that are similar to the programming languages in which they write, instead of the abstractions that non-technical people are comfortable with. Without speaking to non-technical people about their experiences with computers, coders all but ignore them in the development of their software.  

Finally, we list one more deficiency that is near and dear to my heart: quality. The quality of open-source software often leaves a lot to be desired. The software evolves way too rapidly, often has tons of bugs in most early stages of development, and if your task is to just get your work done, and not spend your time fixing the open-source tools that you use, you just spin your wheels, waiting for show-stopping bugs to get fixed every other week. Personally, I’ve had it with GCC and any UNIX-compatible shells on Windows. They just don’t work correctly for all the code and Makefiles I want to write and use. For people like me, who care more about their own project than the open-source tools we use, open-source will always remain a frustrated source of code that’s too complex and time-consuming to “fix” on my own, and isn’t fixed fast enough to get my work done.
