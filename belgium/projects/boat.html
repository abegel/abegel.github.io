Boat Teacher's Resource

In this project, students master the concept of relative movement through experimention with a simple computer model.


Grades: 5 and up
Type: Short to Medium-term

Try to direct the boat with and against the current. Experiment with different settings for the current velocity and the boat's own speed.

  • With the current: Observe that overall, the boat's speed depends on the current velocity.
  • Against the stream: Can you make the boat stand still in the current?  Try to set the curent's velocity and  the boat's speed so that the river sweeps the boat down.


Grades: 5 and up
Type: Medium-term

The following  is a simple way to enrich the project so that it allows quantitative experiments.

Have two piers on the river. You can draw them directly onto the background or use two turtles to represent them. It is better if you know the distance between the piers from the beginning, but you can always find it later. Hint: use the turtle's ability for exact motion to draw the piers at a particular distance from each other.

Now, have a counter to measure the "time" - number of steps - the boat travels from one point to another. Even if your students are not experienced programmers, they can suggest various ways to create a counter in a MicroWorlds project. Some of their ideas may strike you as very original. For example, the following idea was suggested by a fourth-grade girl:  Have a second turtle who's heading will change by one degree each time the boat takes a step.

Discuss with your students how the same counter can be used to measure the distance the boat has traveled (this is the way to find the distance between the piers).

Program a pier (a color or a turtle) to stop the boat or to turn it, and to stop or reset your counter.

You are now all set for an experiment! Have the students estimate the time it takes the boat to travel from one pier to another and then check their results experimentally. Are the results always identical? Why?
