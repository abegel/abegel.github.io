Inspiral Teacher's Resource

A spiral is an example of a fixed instruction sequence being repeated, while incrementing the length each time to form a spiraling pattern. An inspiral increases the angle each time, while keeping the length fixed. Inspiral is an example of an open-ended problem where the results will not be clear, but may provoke long discussions.


Grades: 6 to 8
Type: Medium to long-term

Inspiral's effects are puzzling. Ask students to keep the angle constant and to try different numbers with inc (increment). They then can try keeping inc constant and varying the angle. If they keep a record of their findings, they may begin to see a pattern. For example, do numbers which divide evenly into 360 give similar results to others that don't? See Abelson and DiSessa (Turtle Geometry, 1981) for a discussion of this open-ended problem.


Grades: 6 to 8
Type: Short-term

Students can make their own spirals with a procedure. Encourage them to add other variables like changing the color. Challenge them to create their own dotted spiral, and to change variables such as subtracting insteading adding an increment. What will happen if they put an instruction after the recursive line?
