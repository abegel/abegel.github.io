Modeling Complex Systems

I'm interested in using parallel computers to model complex systems. Modeling relatively static things like stress on bridges is nice, but I prefer systems that have easily expressible simple rules, instead of average calculations using differential equations.

I'm also very interesting in biology, more from an ecology point of view than from molecular biology and protein folding (which for some reason is becoming all the rage). I'm interested in the evolution of species, ecologies and morphologies (and since a computer is much faster than the real thing, it's an excellent experimentation platform).

StarLogo

I think that parallel agent simulations are pretty cool. I did some work at MIT with the Epistemology and Learning group at the Media Lab with StarLogo, a parallel programming environment for kids. It lets kids program thousands of Logo turtles to run around the screen. We've had kids make simulations of ecological systems (ants, birds, disease, slime molds), social systems (traffic, population segregation), and physics (waves, ideal gases, gravity).

This StarLogo page has pointers to lots of other agent simulations on the web.

Commentary

StarLogo was originally implemented on the CM2 in *Lisp. The CM2 was a SIMD data parallel system, and StarLogo implemented three types of parallel models on top of it. Logo turtles were SPMD, Logo patches (the background was a grid of patches) were SIMD, and the observer was sequential.

While the SIMD and sequential models were a match for the SIMD CM2, SPMD was a little constrained. On a SIMD computer, all processors execute in lockstep, which made for some strange turtle behavior. If turtles were looking for food, the turtle that didn't find any couldn't move until the turtle who had found some picked it up and ate it.

Reimplemented on a set of virtual machines running on a sequential processor allowed us to make the turtle machine truly SPMD. All of the turtles were free to wander in different sections of the code at any time, forcing us to rewrite our example problems over again. :)

StarLogo's problem is scalability. It doesn't scale past one StarLogo world running on one single computer. Its computing model doesn't allow for hierarchical simulation structure, nor does it provide the user with a cost model for execution. On a single computer, this doesn't matter, but implemented on something like a Clump, different worlds would interact with varying speeds.

At one point I did an experiment to evolve rabbits in an ecosystem. Two computers ran the ecosystems in famine and plenty modes. After some time, a wormhole was opened between the two worlds, allowing the rabbits to directly compete with one another. After evolving to their own world, the invader rabbits from the famine world did better in the plenty world than vice versa. In addition, the rabbits from the world running on the Mac IIfx did much worse than the ones on the PowerMac 8100 because the faster computer bred more rabbits in a shorter time. This was a limited experiment, but given enough computers and enough power, a large variety of ecologies could be developed and explored.

Tierra

This is one of the most famous Artificial Life programs out there. Created by Tom Ray at the U. Delaware (now at HIP Research Lab in Japan), it allows people to model organisms living in a soup. First, you write the genome for an ancestor organism in a pseudo-assembly language. This organism can find food and reproduce itself by copying. After filling up the soup with these creatures, you let the simulation go. After some time and random mutation, you start seeing alternative organisms that made mistakes in copying. They're very similar to the ancestor. At some point, though, you start to get parasitic mutants who are much shorter in length, but use assembly code from other normal organisms to copy themselves! It's really exciting stuff.

Tierra's been implemented on workstations, but was also ported to the CM5 and to a distributed computing framework. This Network Tierra is really neat because it's able to model a rapidly mutating distributed ecology with millions of organisms running in parallel and moving themselves around the network.

Swarm

At the Santa Fe Institute, they've got a project called Swarm. This project, from Chris Langton, is a grown-up version of StarLogo that provies a lot more general, flexible and customizable environment.

Sugarscape

Joshua Epstein and Robert Axtell has applied these types of ideas toward modeling social systems. "Sugarscape includes population dynamics, migration, combat, trade, cultural transmission, genetics, environmental effects, immunology, and epidemiology in a spatially distributed artificial society of heterogeneous agents with limited information, evolving preferences, and other recognizably human capabilities and limitations."

EcoLab

This project, by Russell Standish at UNSW in Australia explores Lotke-Volterra interactions between populations of organisms.
